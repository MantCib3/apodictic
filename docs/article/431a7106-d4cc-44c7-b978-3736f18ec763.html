UN trade agency says it faces 'painful' cuts as countries navigate tariffs

June 18, 2025
  • UNCTAD faces 'painful' budget cuts.
  • Cuts threaten aid for developing countries.
  • U.S. tariffs disrupt global trade frameworks.
  • 2025 budget may shrink by 15%.
The United Nations Conference on Trade and Development (UNCTAD) is facing significant budget cuts due to declining global donor funding, exacerbated by complex tariff policies, its secretary general announced. These ‘painful’ reductions threaten UNCTAD’s mission to support developing countries in accessing global markets. The agency, established to promote equitable trade, is grappling with financial constraints as nations prioritize domestic economic policies amid U.S.-led tariff hikes. The cuts could reduce programs aiding least-developed countries, particularly in Africa and Asia, where trade access is critical for growth. Secretary General Rebeca Grynspan highlighted the challenge of maintaining support for vulnerable economies during a period of global trade uncertainty. The announcement comes as U.S. tariffs, intensified since President Trump’s January 2025 return, have disrupted supply chains, prompting retaliatory measures from countries like China. This has strained multilateral trade frameworks, impacting UNCTAD’s operations. Additional research indicates that UNCTAD’s 2025 budget may shrink by up to 15%, limiting technical assistance and capacity-building initiatives. Developing nations, reliant on UNCTAD for trade policy guidance, face increased risks of economic isolation. Grynspan emphasized the need for renewed international cooperation to stabilize funding. Posts on X reflect concern among analysts about the ripple effects on global trade equity. The agency is exploring alternative funding models, but prospects remain uncertain as geopolitical tensions and protectionist policies persist.
